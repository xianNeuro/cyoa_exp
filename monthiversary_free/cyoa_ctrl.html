1. Research Summary (Key Information): The information in this section is intended to be an introduction to the study only. Details of the study are listed in the sections below. You can ask questions about the study now and at any time in the future, by contacting us at jchenlab@jhu.edu.

The purpose of this study is to examine how memory and perceptual functions interact in the brain. We are interested in how the brain enables us to perceive streams of information over time, and how people remember these experiences.

Participation in this study will typically take 15-60 minutes. Participants are required to attend and respond to a variety of stimuli, including sequences of spoken language, voiced syllables, and visual cues. There are no known physical or psychological risks associated with your participation in this study, other than the possibility of fatigue, boredom, or slight discomfort. The only cost for participation is a participant’s time. The benefit of participating in this study is to be involved in the pursuit of knowledge and understanding of the human brain. Participants are compensated at a flat rate or via course credit.


2. Why is this research being done?:

This research is being done to examine how memory and perceptual functions interact in the brain. In particular, we are interested how the brain enables us to perceive streams of information over time, and how people remember these experiences.

People between the ages of 18-65 who speak English may join.


3. What will happen if you join this study?

If you agree to be in this study, we will ask you to do the following things:

You will participate in a computerized task. You will see a series of images or short movies on the screen or may hear a series of sounds or words. Your task is to carefully examine each image or movie, or to carefully listen to each sequence of sounds, so that you can report what you saw or heard.

Alternatively, you will engage in common web-surfing experiences such as reading news articles, planning routes on mapping websites, locating music videos, playing Tetris, and/or having text-based conversations with other participants.

Sometimes we will ask you to directly report what you saw or heard, for example by providing a yes/no response (e.g. "Was the image moving?" or "Did you hear the same sound twice in a row?"). In other cases, we may ask you to make a judgment about images (e.g. "Which images belong together?") or about other features of the stimuli (e.g. "Which sound was louder?"). In still other cases, we may ask you to make a prediction about the stimuli (e.g. “What would you expect to hear or see next?”). You may be tested on your memory of what you saw or heard, asked to provide written descriptions of the task, or asked to provide short descriptions of life events.

After completing the main experiment, we will ask you to ﬁll out a brief questionnaire that asks about some optional personal information (e.g., your age) and gives you an opportunity to comment on your experience performing the task.

The study will typically take 20-60 minutes to complete. The first phase of the experiment will typically take 10-30 minutes. The memory and questionnaire phases of the experiment will each take 5-10 minutes.

The order and tasks assigned to each participant may differ. The tasks seen by each participant are chosen at random, like a coin flip. Additionally, participants may see different stimuli, and the order and selection of these stimuli are chosen at random, like drawing numbers from a hat.

4. What are the risks or discomforts of the study?

You may get tired or bored when we are asking you questions, or when you are completing questionnaires. There is a risk when answering these questions that information about you may become known to people outside this study. We collect demographics information including but not limited to, race, ethnicity, gender, handedness, and age of language acquisition. Please note that all demographic questions are optional and are not required to successfully participate in this study. In online studies, personally identifiable information linked to your user ID may be collected via the online experiment platform providers (e.g., Amazon, Prolific). However, identifiable information will be de-coupled from online IDs to decrease the risk of linking this information with your person. This information may be shared with the NIH’s NDA and other databases, but only after de-identification. In studies that involve screen recording, there may be a risk to your privacy if the screen recording contains any private information. You should remove any and all personally identifying information from the screen prior to the experiment. You also have the right to withdraw your screen recording at any time during the experiment in case any information that you do not want to share is exposed during the experiment. Additionally, some of the texts, images, audio stories, or movies used in this experiment may contain mature themes that can be inappropriate for children under the age of 17. These mature themes may cause slight discomfort. If you feel uncomfortable at any time, due to these mature themes or for any other reason, you have the right to stop the experiment.

Aside from this, the risks associated with participation in this study are no greater than those encountered in daily life or during the performance of routine physical or psychological examinations or tests.

11. How will the confidentiality of your biospecimens and/or data be protected?

Any study records that identify you will be kept conﬁdential to the extent possible by law. The records from your participation may be reviewed by people responsible for making sure that research is done properly, including members of the Johns Hopkins University Homewood Institutional Review Board and ofﬁcials from government agencies such as the National Institutes of Health and the Ofﬁce for Human Research Protections (all of these people are required to keep your identity conﬁdential). Otherwise, records that identify you will be available only to people working on the study, unless you give permission for other people to see the records. The experimental data and questionnaire responses collected during your participation will be anonymized.

De-identiﬁed and anonymized data (e.g. response times, key presses, and questionnaire responses) may be made freely available to the scientiﬁc community to encourage scientiﬁc openness and reproducibility. This sharing of anonymous data may include depositing data in open data repositories, such as https://openneuro.org, or in curated data repositories, such as the National Institute of Mental Health Data Archive (NDA). If you would like more information about NDA, this is available online at https://nda.nih.gov.

Under some experimental conditions, the anonymized and de-identiﬁed raw data, such as generated word associations, may also be shared with other participants. In such cases, the raw data will be manually scrubbed of any personally identiﬁable information. If you have any concerns about sharing of anonymized data, please do not participate.
